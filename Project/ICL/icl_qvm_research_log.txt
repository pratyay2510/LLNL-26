Abstract
This document captures a comprehensive research discussion bridging modern In-Context
Learning (ICL) in Large Language Models with classical Query Vector Movement (QVM) the-
ories. It includes a literature review of recent ICL advancements, a structured reading guide, a
detailed breakdown of ICL mechanics, and a formal synthesis connecting attention dynamics to
relevance feedback, concluding with proposed ablation studies.

Recent Literature on In-Context Learning (ICL)
Query: Give me the most recent papers on In Context Learning (ICL).
Here are some of the most significant recent papers on In-Context Learning (ICL), highlighting
new methodologies, theoretical frameworks, and applications in multi-modal architectures:
• Many-Shot CoT-ICL: Making In-Context Learning Truly Learn (Chung, n.d.): In-
vestigates ICL behavior when scaled to dozens or hundreds of examples. Proposes Curvilinear
Demonstration Selection (CDS) to restructure the context window into a learning curriculum
for chain-of-thought reasoning.
• Parallel In-context Learning for Large Vision Language Models (Yamaguchi,
n.d.): Tackles the quadratic computational cost of multi-modal ICL. The Parallel-ICL al-
gorithm partitions contexts, processes chunks in parallel, and merges predictions using a
weighted Product-of-Experts ensemble.
• On Theoretical Interpretations of Concept-Based In-Context Learning (Tang et
al., 2025): Introduces a mathematical framework quantifying how models leverage prompt
knowledge and proposes a similarity measure between demonstrations and queries.
Foundational context is provided by Kossen et al. (2023) on label relationships, Lin & Lee
(2024) on dual operating modes, and Dong et al. (2022) for a comprehensive general survey.

Recommended Reading Progression
Query: Give me a rough order of reading since I want to learn about the topic and then look at
recent advancements.
To understand ICL from the ground up, the following progression is recommended:
1. Core Foundations: The Birth of ICL
• Language Models are Few-Shot Learners (Brown et al., 2020): The seminal GPT-3 paper
introducing ICL.
• It’s Not Just Size That Matters: Small Language Models Are Also Few-Shot Learners (Schick
& Schütze, 2021): Introduces Pattern-Exploiting Training (PET) for smaller models.
2. Surveys & Syntheses: Mapping the Landscape
• A Survey on In-context Learning (Dong et al., 2022): The definitive early survey formalizing
ICL definitions and prompt design.
• A Practical Survey on Zero-shot Prompt Design for In-context Learning (Li, 2023): Focuses
on discrete and continuous prompts.
3. Advanced Mechanics: How Does it Actually Work?
• Rethinking the Role of Demonstrations (Min et al., 2022): Argues that demonstrations pri-
marily teach format and label space, not the input-output mapping.
• In-Context Learning Learns Label Relationships but Is Not Conventional Learning (Kossen et
al., 2023): Provides a counter-argument that ICL does rely on label information in certain
scenarios.
4. The Current Frontier: Many-Shot & Multi-Modal (2024–2026)
• A Survey on Multimodal Large Language Models (Yin et al., 2023): Covers the transition to
Multimodal ICL (MM-ICL).
• Many-Shot In-Context Learning (Agarwal et al., 2024): Explores scaling to thousands of
demonstrations.
• From Few to Many: Self-Improving Many-Shot Reasoners (Wan et al., 2025): Explores au-
tomated prompt construction and demonstration optimization.

1. Introduction & Formulation
Intuition: ICL allows models to learn without parameter updates, functioning similarly to an
amnesiac student recognizing patterns from a few solved examples on a worksheet.
Math: In ICL, parameters θ are frozen. A context C = {(x1, y1), . . . , (xk, yk)} is concatenated

2. Prompt Design
The exact composition of C is paramount, divided into three sub-problems:
• Demonstration Selection: Finding examples conceptually closest to the target query using
unsupervised distance metrics (e.g., L2, cosine similarity) or supervised retrieval models.
• Demonstration Formatting: Mapping inputs and labels into a natural language template
(discrete prompts) or vector space (continuous prompts).
• Demonstration Ordering: Mitigating recency bias, where models place disproportionate
weight on the final example preceding the query.
3. Inference & Scoring Strategies
Math: Contrasting direct scoring with channel scoring to mitigate prior probability bias:
Direct Scoring: Score = P(y|x, C)
(2)
Channel Scoring: Score = P(x|y, C) · P(y|C)
(3)
Channel scoring naturally normalizes output distribution and mitigates bias toward common words.
4. Pre-training for ICL
ICL capabilities scale with pre-training corpora containing long-range dependencies and implicit
analogies. Meta-tuning on ICL-formatted data can further sharpen pattern recognition.
